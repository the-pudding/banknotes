Who's in Your Wallet?

Analyzing more than 200 portraits on banknotes across the globe can give us a better picture of countries' history and national identity

March XX, 2022
Story & Data
Alejandra Arevalo & Eric Hausken
Code
Jeff MacInnes

While it seems that we're moving toward a cashless society, the value of paper money can't be underestimated. People without smartphones, without bank accounts or without reliable internet are some of the people who rely on cash to perform their daily transactionsâ€“and removing paper money from the streets would mean isolating them from the economic system. Even if you normally use digital payment methods, we bet you carry some cash in your wallet to buy food from your favorite street vendor, access a cash-only discount at a store, in case your phone dies or you've maxed out your credit card.

In fact, if you open your wallet right now, who do you see there? (This is just a thought experiment, we don't need your credit card information, don't worry). You might see some of America's founders like George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, Abraham Lincoln or Alexander Hamilton. Maybe you see former heads of state Andrew Jackson or Ulysses S. Grant.

And if you're reading this from , you're probably looking at people who made history in your country. Without even noticing, you're always carrying around reminders of prominent people in your wallet, but have you ever wondered, "who gets to be on banknotes?"

Beyond their utilitarian purpose, the banknotes in your wallet encapsulate the history, language and culture of the place you live in, including the larger social and institutional issues that countries battle with everyday. Many countries have an overwhelming representation of aristocratic men in banknotes, relegating to the sidelines the faces of women and people from racial and ethnic minorities that have been just as instrumental (if not more so) to their country's development.

Some countries are trying to make the future of banknotes much more representative of their societies through nationwide design contests and diverse selection committees. But as you'll see in our analysis, we still have a long way to go to achieve equitable representation in the banknotes of the world.

We decided to investigate the people who appear on banknotes around the world, to see what we could learn about them and their countries. We gathered the data from the official websites of national central banks, the Standard Catalog of World Paper Money, 25th Edition, Wikipedia's list of people on banknotes , and multiple biographies about the individuals.

We wanted this analysis to be as international as possible so we inquired into countries from all 22 sub and sub-subregions of the world, based on the United Nations' Statistics Department geoscheme. Out of the 22 regions, we found that 21 had countries with currencies that met our criteria (read methodology at the end) so our study includes at least one country per region. The only region not in our analysis was Western Europe because none of its countries include people in their circulating banknotes.

In total, we analyzed 243 unique people on banknotes from 38 countries. Fifty-three were women (22% of portraits) and 190 were men. They appear on 236 unique bills. Why are the numbers of unique people and unique banknotes different? Well, several countries feature the same person on multiple banknotes like Bangladesh, which has their founder in all nine of their denominations. For the purpose of counting unique people, Bangladesh's founder would be counted as a single person but he would count as nine unique bills. Other countries have multiple portraits in one banknote, like the 10 CAD banknote which includes the portraits of four prominent Canadians. Each Canadian would be counted as a unique person but together would count as a single bill.

We looked at their professions and accomplishments, among other characteristics, to understand who is on the banknotes of the world, and what it took to get there.

Writing influential work can get you on a banknote

Coming into this analysis, we suspected that the people on banknotes would mostly be heads of government and monarchs, since they hold so much power and oftentimes have the last word on economic matters. We were surprised to find that writers were the most popular choice. But of course, they aren't just any writers. The writers on banknotes are influential thinkers who shaped their countries' literature and whose work is known by heart and praised by many.

Nineteen percent of the people featured on banknotes were Writers (45 people from 23 countries) which makes writing the most common profession among currency individuals. Many of those classified as writers have a diverse portfolio which spans from poetry and fiction to journalism and academic writing, so we opted for classifying them as writers at large. Writing is the most common profession among women (14 women) and the second most common for men (31 men).

Israel, Kyrgyzstan and Ukraine have the most writers among their currency with four writers each. In fact, Israel only features Hebrew poets in their current banknotes. According to the Bank of Israel, the New Shekel "carry portraits of outstanding Hebrew poets whose life stories, works, and activities are intertwined with the story of the rebirth of the Nation of Israel in its land."

The New Shekel is an example of radical changes in banknote imagery throughout the years. Before the Shekel was founded , the first Israeli Lira depicted landscapes and the second series depicted "drawings of people illustrating different walks of Israeli life." It was not until the third series in 1970 that there were portraits of specific people from history. From 1980 to 2014, the Israeli Shekel showed portraits of five former heads of government.

The designer of the current Israeli banknotes, Osnat Eshel, told newspaper Haaretz that she was "brought up with Hebrew poetry at home" and that's why she felt so dearly about creating designs inspired by their widely known poems. This example could indicate that citizens might have deep emotional connections with the works of writers which makes them a great choice to represent national identity on banknotes.

However, Amit Trainin, head of the Visual Communication Department at Tel Aviv's Minshar for Art, has a different take on why writers are a subject of choice for banknotes, telling Haaretz: "if we're going to have bombastic national symbols [on banknotes], better poor poets than rich generals."

While notable writers might seem like a safe choice for banknotes, their selection can still perpetuate divisions in the population. Many decried that the current Israeli series only represented Ashkenazi Jews and didn't include anyone of Sephardi or Mizrahi heritage, an exclusion that some hope will be solved in following editions.

Being a state leader or country's founder can get you on a bill

Among the banknotes we analyzed, the second most common profession was Head of Government. There are 37 male heads of governments from 18 countries on banknotes (20% of all male characters). However, we found that only one female head of government is featured on a banknote, Corazon C. Aquino. She was president of the Philippines from 1986 to 1992 and is known for restoring democracy after the dictatorship of Ferdinand Marco.

While the heads of governments are politicians, we created another category called Politician for those who were involved in politics but never led their nation. Politician was the fourth most common profession for men, and the fifth for women. There are only four female politicians featured on banknotes and three of them were the first women to hold a specific government role:

  • Rose Lomathinda Chibambo, the first female cabinet minister of Malawi
  • Agnes Macphail and Edith Cowan, the first elected women to the parliaments of Canada and Australia respectively

Another category that lacked female representation was Founders. Not a single country we analyzed featured a woman who is considered the founder of their nation. The United States was the country that featured the most founders with four: George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, Alexander Hamilton and Benjamin Franklin.

Instead, women who were involved in independence wars are often called "National Heroes" or revolutionaries. For example, the Venezuelan central bank writes that Josefa Camejo is a heroine of the independence movement, while the descriptions of her male contemporaries pictured on the banknotes include "father of the nation" and "leader". Indeed, Revolutionary was the second most common occupation among women on banknotes.

We didn't count Monarchs as heads of government because oftentimes they don't play that role so we gave them their own category. This is how we found that monarch isn't a top-ranked profession among banknote characters. There are just 12 monarchs featured on banknotes and only two of them are women: Queen Elizabeth II and Queen Tamar of Georgia.

We acknowledge that heads of government, politicians, founders and monarchs are all involved in governance in some shape or form and therefore, could be counted together. If we were to add up the four categories it would yield 103 unique people. This means that people involved in governance are in 42% of all the personalities we analyzed. Writer would still be a top-ranked profession, coming in second place.

It helps to be known as the first person to do something

Being the first to do something can immediately turn you into a memorable historical figure. The first one of your demographic to be a member of Congress, the first president of a nation, the first Indigenous person to graduate from a national university. These are just some of the "firsts" that some of our characters achieved during their lifetimes.

For the purpose of this analysis, a person is considered first to do something if it is explicitly mentioned in their biographies (official government websites, Wikipedia, Encyclopedia Britannica, etc.) and if that act contributes to their fame. For example, Agnes Macphail was Canada's first female member of parliament and her face graces the 10 CAD banknote. If one searches for her name online, this fact is mentioned in almost every website that talks about her.

On average, 29% of people in a country's currency were known for being the first ones to do something. This means that on an average country's banknote series, nearly two people are notable firsts. Some notable firsts include Edmund Hillary of New Zealand, the first to reach the peak of Mount Everest, and Alhaji Aliyu Mai-Bornu of Nigeria, the first indigeneous Governor of the Central Bank of Nigeria.

All four countries that only had one person on all their banknote denominations were notable firsts:

  • Mao Zedong, the first Chairman of China
  • Mujibur Rahman, the first President of Bangladesh
  • Michael Somare, the first Prime Minister of Papua New Guinea
  • Nelson Mandela, the first President of South Africa

Both New Zealand and Nigeria include the most trailblazers. Other than the countries above with only one portrait, New Zealand has the largest percentage of notable firsts and Nigeria has the most notable firsts in total. Four out of five people on the NZD were notable firsts. These people come from a diverse range of professions as well. Among them are politician Apirana Ngata, the first Maori person to graduate from a New Zealand university, and chemist Lord Rutherford of Nelson, the first person to win the Nobel prize from Oceania. Kate Shepard, a prominent suffragette, the founder and first president of the National Council of Women, is also pictured on the NZD bill.

Six of the nine people on the Nigerian naira were notable firsts. Other than the economists Alhaji Aliyu Mai-Bornu and Dr. Clement Isong, whose portraits are on the N1000, the other trailblazers were heads of government and founding fathers. They include Dr. Nnamdi Azikiwe, the first President, and Alhaji Sir Abubarkar Tafawa Balewa, the first Prime Minister.

We found that the median bill values for both male and female characters correspond to 100 monetary units (dollar, peso, krona, etc). This is notable because it shows that, on average, male and female portraits were not significantly different from each other in monetary value. Similar to gender, there is no apparent correlation between profession and bill value.

However, there are some countries that are intentional about who they put in a specific bill value. This is the case of Sweden as they thoughtfully decided that Astrid Lindgren, the author of the children's books about Pippi Longstocking, would be in the 20-note krona.

Unfortunately, you probably won't see yourself on a banknote in your lifetime

Only ten people were alive to see themselves on banknotes. The remaining 96% of the portraits were issued after the person had died.

Of all the people who died before they were issued, it normally took decades until their portrait appeared on a banknote. For exactly â…“ of the people, it took centuries. Eighty-one people died more than 100 years before they were issued to a current legal-tender banknote. Hannibal, the famous Military Commander from ancient Carthage and face of the Tunisian dinar, died in 183 BCE, which makes him the oldest person in our dataset.

South Korea had the oldest people on average: all four of them lived in the 15th and 16th centuries.
After South Korea, the country with the second highest average wait is Ukraine, the 10 people on their banknotes have an average wait of 319 years. Two of them were medieval Monarchs who died in the 11th century. The most recent person on Ukraine's currency was Volodymyr Vernadskyi, a scientist who died in 1945 and was issued a banknote in 2019, a 74-year wait.
The shortest waiting time for someone to be put on a banknote after their death was one year, and this was only the case for four people: Gabriel GarcÃ­a MÃ¡rquez of Colombia, General Murtala Mohammed of Nigeria, Corazon C. Aquino and Manuel A. Roxas of the Philippines. All four of these people were notable firsts as well. We found that people who were known for a first were issued on a banknote sooner than those that were not notable firsts.

Some countries have gone a step further by creating laws that prohibit living people from being featured on bills. The United States Treasury makes it clear that only deceased persons are legally allowed to be on the currency bills. The Bank of England does not include people who are fictional or alive, except for the Monarch. Similarly, aside from the Monarch, the Bank of Canada requires that the person must be a non-fictional Canadian and must have been dead for at least 25 years.

Some final thoughts

Will the designs of paper money matter in the coming decades as we move towards more virtual transactions and less cash exchange? We can't predict the future, but as a historical artifact, banknotes can tell us a lot about the values of the people in power and who they considered national symbols.

In many cases, paper money still fails to represent the people it serves. People like us are unlikely to find ourselves on a banknote. We may be writers, but until we win the Nobel, our odds are slim. Right now, the average person would be better off being the first to achieve something remarkable, going into politics, leading a rebellion, or founding their own country. That said, the reward will likely come after you die.

Even then, introducing (or refusing to introduce) a new person to a bill can be controversial, oftentimes, a political move or propaganda that tries to legitimize a specific historical character or regime. However, there are benefits that come with having people on money, if we do it with inclusivity in mind. Money can be a platform to share the history of our nations and uplift unsung leaders who deserve our gratitude for making our countries what they are.

A few governments are attempting to do this. Four countries portray an activist and nine others portray revolutionaries who fought to overthrow their oppressors. The Mirabal sisters, pictured on the Dominican Peso, created a political movement that opposed the former dictator's sexist and violent policies and fought for civil rights. Tragically, they were assassinated by the dictator's henchmen in 1960. In 2007, 47 years later, the Mirabal sisters were introduced to the peso as symbols of democratic and feminist resistance.

The U.S. Treasury is trying to address some of its racist and sexist history by introducing Harriet Tubman in replacement of Andrew Jackson on the $20 bill. However, this attempt was delayed by former president Donald Trump, who is reportedly a fan of the seventh American president. Indeed, the delay was a political move as Trump admitted that he was against changing the bill because he considers this change "pure political correctness."

To avoid controversies, many currencies are moving away from portraits to instead display architecture, flora and fauna in their bills. Some currencies that have made that change include the Danish krone, the Euro and the Swiss Franc.

It seems that the designers of the Euro banknotes understood the problems with portraying specific people and national symbols as they were trying to create a currency that would be shared among dozens of countries. According to the European Central Bank, "The new Europa series banknotes show architectural styles from various periods in Europe's history, but do not show any actual existing monuments or bridges."

While it's unclear whether more currencies will move towards faceless banknotes, their legacy and historical value will remain a subject of study and a source of heated discourse. After all, these little pieces of paper symbolize power itselfâ€“spending power, political power, ideological power and more.

Methods

Since we wanted to learn more about the specific people on banknotes, we narrowed our investigation to banknotes with non-generic people on them. This means that the country has explicitly said that the portrait on the banknote belongs to a specific person and it's not an unspecified representation of a villager, a woman, a fictional character, etc.

Another condition we followed was to only count circulating banknotes that aren't being recalled. This way we are only looking at bills that are and will continue to be legal tender for the foreseeable future.

We drew inspiration from a 2016 article by Vox about women on world banknotes. Since then, the data has changed so we collected updated numbers for this article.

Please remember that the conclusions and statistics presented in this article are only applicable for the countries analyzed. Collecting updated data on world banknotes is difficult because there isn't a centralized governing body that keeps up-to-date records. Another limitation was that we are only proficient in English and Spanish but some countries' central bank websites weren't available in those languages so we couldn't cross-reference the data.

If you would like to do extra research on the topic, refer to Standard Catalog of World Paper Money 25th Edition or Wikipedia's list of people on banknotes but again, remember these resources aren't updated all the time and you might have to cross-reference with other resources. Many countries keep thorough records of their money on their central bank's website so that is a good resource too.
